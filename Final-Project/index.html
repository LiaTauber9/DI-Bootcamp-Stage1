Martha Graham

Revolt and passion

Dancer and choreographer.
The mother of modern dance.

Biography

Martha Graham's impact on dance has been compared to that of
Picasso's on painting, Stravinsky's on music,
and Frank Lloyd Wright's on architecture.

1984

Born in 1894 in Allegheny County, Pennsylvania

1911 - 1923

In 1911, the ballet dancer Ruth St. Denis performed at the Mason Opera House in Los Angeles.
Inspired by St. Denis’ performance, Graham enrolled in an arts-oriented junior college
and later (1916) to the newly opened Denishawn School. Over eight years, as both a student and an instructor, Graham made Denishawn her home.

1923 - 1926

By 1923, She found her chance dancing in the vaudeville revue Greenwich Village Follies.
At the Greenwich Village Follies, Graham was able to design and choreograph her own dances.
Then she took a position at the Eastman School of Music, where she was free of the constraints of public performance.
At Eastman, Graham was given complete control over her classes and the entire dance program. Graham saw this as an opportunity to engage her best pupils in the experiential dance she was beginning to create.

1927

For Graham, ballet’s concern with flow and grace left behind more violent traditional passions. Graham believed that through spastic movements, tremblings, and falls she could express emotional and spiritual themes ignored by other dance.
She desired to evoke strong emotions, and achieved these visceral responses through the repetition of explicitly sexual and violently disjunctive movements.
Beginning with her Eastman students, she formed the now famous Martha Graham School for Contemporary Dance in New York (1927).

1948 - 1954

In 1948 Graham and Hawkins married, but the marriage was short-lived.
They continued to work together for a while and then made a permanent break.

1965

In 1965 when baroness Batsheva de Rothchild moved to Israel and founded the “Batsheva” dance trope.
She called Graham, her long-time close friendwho became the first director of the troupe.
Graham trained the first generation of dancers in it and created several works for “Batsheva” dance trope.

1969 - 1991

In 1969 that Graham announced her retirement from the stage.
For Graham, however, life away from dance was impossible.
Though no longer able to perform she continued to teach and choreograph until her death in 1991.
